# SquashVote — Brand Guidelines

## Brand personality

**Three words:** Opinionated. Informed. Sharp.

SquashVote is the cheeky commentator who doesn't take sides but loves stirring the pot. It assumes you know the sport. It doesn't explain, apologize, or try to welcome everyone. It respects your time, your knowledge, and your opinion — then shows you exactly where you stand against the crowd and the ref.

Not warm. Not playful. Not corporate. Not slick.

---

## Voice & tone

### How SquashVote speaks

- **Direct.** No filler words, no marketing fluff. Say what it is.
- **Assumes competence.** Never explain what a let is. Never say "learn more about squash rules."
- **Dry wit, not jokes.** The .wtf domain IS the joke. Everything else is delivered straight.
- **Confident.** Statements, not suggestions. "Here's the call" not "Why not try voting?"
- **Short.** If it can be said in 4 words, don't use 8.

### Examples

| Instead of this | Write this |
|----------------|------------|
| "Welcome to SquashVote! Ready to get started?" | "Here's the call. What's yours?" |
| "You've successfully cast your vote!" | "Your call: Stroke." |
| "Check out what other people are saying in the comments!" | "12 comments" |
| "Would you like to try another decision?" | "Next decision" |
| "Upload a video for the community to vote on!" | "Submit a decision" |
| "No sign-up required to get started!" | Don't say this at all. Just don't have sign-up. |

### Words we use

- **Decision**, not "video" or "clip" (the decision is the point, not the footage)
- **Call**, not "vote" when referring to the user's judgment ("make your call")
- **The ref said**, not "official decision" (it's a person who made a judgment, not an institution)
- **Community**, not "users" or "voters"

### Words we avoid

- "Welcome" / "Get started" / "Try it out" (too inviting — we're not a welcome mat)
- "Fun" / "Exciting" / "Amazing" (we're not selling an experience)
- "Learn" / "Discover" / "Explore" (we're not a classroom)
- "Share" / "Follow" / "Like" (we're not a social network)
- Any emoji in UI copy (the interface is text, not decoration)

## Visual identity

### Design philosophy

The UI should feel like Polymarket's clarity and information density, crossed with the communal energy of a subreddit thread. Clean, confident, no decoration for decoration's sake. Every element earns its place by conveying information or enabling action.

**Guiding principles:**
1. **Information density over whitespace.** Respect the user's time. Show them what matters without making them scroll or click for it.
2. **Structure, not decoration.** The formal structure (clip → options → ref call → crowd split) IS the design. It doesn't need gradients, illustrations, or visual flair on top.
3. **Typography does the work.** Weight, size, and spacing create hierarchy — not color, borders, or backgrounds.
4. **Monochrome with purpose.** Color is reserved for data (vote splits, your call vs ref's call). Everything else is black, white, and gray.

### What it should NOT look like

- **ESPN / Sky Sports** — dark gradients, swooshy graphics, "BREAKING" energy. Performative. The audience sees through it.
- **Duolingo / gamified apps** — rounded, colorful, bouncy. Too playful. This isn't a game.
- **Notion / Linear** — too precious, too polished. We're utilitarian, not beautiful-for-the-sake-of-it.
- **Generic sports betting sites** — garish, loud, distracting. We're the opposite of that.

### Typography

Primary typeface should be:
- **Neutral, sharp, and highly legible.** Think Inter, Space Grotesk, or similar grotesque/geometric sans-serifs.
- **No display fonts** for body content. The content speaks for itself.
- Headings: bold weight, tight letter-spacing. No all-caps except for very short labels.
- Body: regular weight, comfortable line-height for reading comments and arguments.

### Color

The palette is intentionally restrained:

- **Primary surface:** Near-white (#fafafa or similar) for light mode, near-black (#111 or #0f0f0f) for dark mode
- **Primary text:** High contrast against surface. Black on white, white on black.
- **Secondary text:** Medium gray (#666 light mode, #999 dark mode) for metadata, timestamps, labels.
- **Accent:** Reserved for data only. Vote distribution bars, "your call" vs "ref's call" differentiation. Not for buttons or decoration.
- **Interactive elements (buttons, links):** Black/white. Not colored. The action is clear from context, not from color.

### Spacing & layout

- **Dense, not cramped.** Generous enough to be readable, tight enough to show information density.
- **Consistent horizontal padding** across all screens (16-20px on mobile).
- **No decorative spacing.** Every gap between elements should feel intentional and structural.
- **Cards and containers:** Minimal borders (1px, light gray). No shadows. No rounded corners larger than 8px. No background color differentiation unless it conveys meaning.

### Iconography

- Minimal. Most things are conveyed with text.
- When needed: simple line icons, 1.5-2px stroke, monochrome.
- No filled icons, no colored icons, no emoji as UI elements.

## Content rules

### What belongs on SquashVote

- A moment in a squash match where a referee made a decision
- The decision must be genuinely debatable — reasonable people could disagree
- The clip must show enough context to make a judgment

### What doesn't belong

- Clean winners or unforced errors (no decision was made)
- Calls that are obviously correct (no debate to be had)
- Non-interference decisions (e.g., service faults, unless genuinely controversial)
- Highlights, trick shots, or "best of" compilations

---

## Dark mode

Dark mode is not an afterthought — given the audience (people watching squash matches, often in the evening, often on mobile), dark mode may be the primary mode.

- Dark mode should feel native, not inverted. Design for dark first if needed.
- Same information density and structure as light mode.
- Reduce contrast slightly for dark mode (don't use pure white #fff on pure black #000 — use #e5e5e5 on #111).

---

## The .wtf

The domain is part of the brand. It communicates: "can you believe this call?" It's the reaction you have when you see a bad decision. It shouldn't be hidden or downplayed. But it also shouldn't be overplayed — the domain does the work on its own. The rest of the UI plays it straight.
